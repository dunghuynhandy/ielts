Cities

Sources: NgocBach Q1 2022, NgocBach Q1 2024, NgocBach Q2 2022 (May–Sep), NgocBach Q3

Key Ideas (IELTS Simon)

Negatives

  • Life in cities has its drawbacks.
  • The cost of living is higher than in rural areas.
  • Some people do not manage to find work.
  • Housing is usually much more expensive.
  • Homelessness and poverty are common in cities.
  • There is a gap between the rich and poor.
  • Life in cities can be extremely stressful.
  • There are problems like traffic congestion and crime.
  • Cities lack a sense of community.
  • People do not even know their neighbours.
  • Cities are sometimes described as “concrete jungles”.
  • Pedestrian Areas
  • Pedestrian zones in city centres can improve the local environment.
  • Banning cars encourages people to walk or cycle.
  • Many European cities have built bicycle lanes.
  • Dependence on cars is linked to health problems like obesity.
  • People who walk or cycle regularly are generally healthier.
  • Pedestrian areas are safer and more attractive for both residents and tourists.

Sample Answers Band 6.5–7.0

Part 1 — Cities

1. Are you interested in urban life?

Yes, to some extent. I would not say I am an expert, but urban life affects daily life, so I find it worth thinking about. For example, when I read news or talk with friends, this topic often comes up naturally.

2. Is urban life important in your country?

Yes, definitely. In my country, urban life matters because it influences families, young people, and the wider community. It may not be something people discuss every day, but it has a real impact on quality of life.

3. Has urban life changed in recent years?

Yes, quite a lot. Technology, education, and modern lifestyles have changed the way people think about urban life. Older people may have more traditional views, while younger people tend to be more open and practical.

4. Do young people care about urban life?

I think many young people do care, especially when it affects their future directly. However, they may express interest through social media or online discussions rather than traditional conversations.

Part 2 — Cue card sample

A modern city i would like to visit

I would like to talk about a modern city I would like to visit, which is closely connected to urban life.

I first became aware of it a few years ago, and since then it has stayed in my mind because it reflects both the positive and negative sides of modern life.

The positive side is that it shows the value of public transport and green spaces. This is important because people often need practical examples, not just abstract ideas, to understand a topic clearly.

At the same time, it also reminds me of the problem of traffic congestion and high living costs. That makes the topic more complex, because there is rarely a perfect solution.

Overall, I find it meaningful because it helps me think more deeply about society and my own choices. If I had to summarise my feeling, I would say it is both interesting and relevant to everyday life.

Part 3 — Discussion

1. Why is urban life important?

It is important because it affects individuals and society at the same time. For example, public transport and green spaces can improve peoples quality of life, while traffic congestion and high living costs can create long-term problems. That is why this topic deserves more attention from both citizens and governments.

2. What are the advantages related to urban life?

The main advantages are connected to public transport and green spaces. If this area is managed well, people can benefit through better opportunities, more convenience, and a higher standard of living. It can also help communities become more organised and forward-thinking.

3. What are the disadvantages or risks?

The biggest risk is traffic congestion and high living costs. If people ignore this issue, it can lead to social pressure, inequality, or environmental damage depending on the situation. In my view, the negative side should not be exaggerated, but it should be taken seriously.

4. What should governments do about urban life?

Governments should create clear policies, invest in public education, and make sure rules are enforced fairly. For example, they can fund useful programmes and raise public awareness. However, government action alone is not enough; individuals also need to change their habits.

5. Do you think urban life will change in the future?

Yes, I think it will continue to change, mainly because of technology, globalisation, and younger generations. People are becoming more aware of both benefits and risks. In the future, I expect more practical solutions rather than simple arguments for or against it.

6. Do older and younger people think differently about urban life?

Yes, in many cases. Older people may value tradition, stability, and personal experience, while younger people are often influenced by online information and global trends. Neither side is completely right or wrong; the best approach is to combine experience with fresh ideas.
