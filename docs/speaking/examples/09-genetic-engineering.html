Genetic Engineering

Sources: by-source mapping (no direct match found yet)

Key Ideas (IELTS Simon)

Positives

  • Genetic engineering is the practice of manipulating the genes of an organism.
  • It is used to produce crops that are more resistant to insects and diseases.
  • Some genetically modified crops grow more quickly.
  • Some drugs and vaccines are produced by genetic engineering.
  • It may become possible to change humans’ genetic characteristics.
  • Scientists may use genetic engineering to cure diseases.
  • Inherited illnesses would no longer exist.
  • Genes could be changed before a baby is born.
  • It could also be possible to clone human organs.
  • We could all have replacement body parts.
  • Humans could live longer, healthier lives.

Negatives

  • There are ethical concerns about human genetic engineering.
  • Parents might want to choose their children’s characteristics.
  • This would be unnatural.
  • It would be unacceptable in most religions.
  • Soldiers could be cloned from the genes of the strongest people.
  • Clones might be used like robots to do certain jobs.
  • Clones might even be developed just for organ replacements.
  • Society and human evolution would change completely.
  • Currently, human genetic engineering is prohibited.
  • Genetically-Modified (GM) Foods: Advantages
  • Farmers can produce crops that grow bigger and faster.
  • Some GM crops are more resistant to disease or insects.
  • This could be important for food production in developing countries.
  • Faster growing cereals, fruit and vegetables will mean more profit.
  • GM foods can be modified to look perfect.
  • They may be more attractive to customers.
  • Genetically-Modified (GM) Foods: Disadvantages
  • There may be risks involved in the genetic engineering of foods.
  • GM crops might change whole ecosystems.
  • Food chains could be broken if crops are resistant to predators.
  • Organic foods are produced without chemicals or genetic modification.
  • Organic farming may be slower and more expensive.
  • However, the environment is not damaged by fertilisers or pesticides.

Part 1 — Genetic Engineering

1. Are you interested in science and food production?

Yes, to some extent. I would not say I am an expert, but science and food production affects daily life, so I find it worth thinking about. For example, when I read news or talk with friends, this topic often comes up naturally.

2. Is science and food production important in your country?

Yes, definitely. In my country, science and food production matters because it influences families, young people, and the wider community. It may not be something people discuss every day, but it has a real impact on quality of life.

3. Has science and food production changed in recent years?

Yes, quite a lot. Technology, education, and modern lifestyles have changed the way people think about science and food production. Older people may have more traditional views, while younger people tend to be more open and practical.

4. Do young people care about science and food production?

I think many young people do care, especially when it affects their future directly. However, they may express interest through social media or online discussions rather than traditional conversations.

Part 2 — Cue card sample

A scientific development that changed the world

I would like to talk about a scientific development that changed the world, which is closely connected to science and food production.

I first became aware of it a few years ago, and since then it has stayed in my mind because it reflects both the positive and negative sides of modern life.

The positive side is that it shows the value of better crops and medical treatment. This is important because people often need practical examples, not just abstract ideas, to understand a topic clearly.

At the same time, it also reminds me of the problem of ethical risks and unknown long-term effects. That makes the topic more complex, because there is rarely a perfect solution.

Overall, I find it meaningful because it helps me think more deeply about society and my own choices. If I had to summarise my feeling, I would say it is both interesting and relevant to everyday life.

Part 3 — Discussion

1. Why is science and food production important?

It is important because it affects individuals and society at the same time. For example, better crops and medical treatment can improve peoples quality of life, while ethical risks and unknown long-term effects can create long-term problems. That is why this topic deserves more attention from both citizens and governments.

2. What are the advantages related to science and food production?

The main advantages are connected to better crops and medical treatment. If this area is managed well, people can benefit through better opportunities, more convenience, and a higher standard of living. It can also help communities become more organised and forward-thinking.

3. What are the disadvantages or risks?

The biggest risk is ethical risks and unknown long-term effects. If people ignore this issue, it can lead to social pressure, inequality, or environmental damage depending on the situation. In my view, the negative side should not be exaggerated, but it should be taken seriously.

4. What should governments do about science and food production?

Governments should create clear policies, invest in public education, and make sure rules are enforced fairly. For example, they can fund useful programmes and raise public awareness. However, government action alone is not enough; individuals also need to change their habits.

5. Do you think science and food production will change in the future?

Yes, I think it will continue to change, mainly because of technology, globalisation, and younger generations. People are becoming more aware of both benefits and risks. In the future, I expect more practical solutions rather than simple arguments for or against it.

6. Do older and younger people think differently about science and food production?

Yes, in many cases. Older people may value tradition, stability, and personal experience, while younger people are often influenced by online information and global trends. Neither side is completely right or wrong; the best approach is to combine experience with fresh ideas.
