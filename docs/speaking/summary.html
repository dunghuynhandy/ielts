IELTS Speaking — Band 6.5 → 7.0 Guide

Target: move from Band 6.5 to 7.0. Use with examples and vocabulary.

Band 6.5 vs 7.0 — what's the difference?

Overall score = average of 4 criteria. At 6.5 you are almost there — usually one criterion holds you back.

Criterion Band 6.5 (typical) Band 7.0 (what to add)
Fluency Can speak at length but with some repetition, self-correction, or hesitation Keeps going without noticeable effort; clear logical flow
Vocabulary Good range on familiar topics; some awkward word choices Flexible word choice; some less common words + collocations used naturally
Grammar Mix of simple/complex; errors don't always block meaning Frequent error-free sentences; complex structures used with flexibility
Pronunciation Clear enough; occasional unclear words Easy to understand throughout; natural word stress

The jump from 6.5 → 7.0 is usually about: 1. Length — answers still a bit short 2. Structure — ideas present but not organised 3. Part 3 depth — still too personal, not enough "big picture"

You do not need native-level English. You need longer, clearer, slightly more advanced answers.

Answer length targets (Band 6.5 → 7.0)

Part Band 6.5 (too short) Band 7.0 (target)
Part 1 1–2 sentences 3–4 sentences (15–25 seconds)
Part 2 1–1.5 minutes 2 minutes — all bullets covered
Part 3 2–3 sentences, mostly "I think…" 4–6 sentences — opinion + reason + example + result

Frameworks (use every practice session)

Part 1 — EXTEND or EPACT

EXTEND: Answer → Detail → Reason/Example

EPACT (habits, preferences, technology): | Step | Add… | |------|------| | Example | Something from your life | | Popularity | How common it is | | Advantage | A positive | | Cost | A downside | | Time | Past habit or future plan |

Part 2 — ISO

INTRO   (15 sec)   → "I'd like to talk about…"
STORY   (75 sec)   → Every bullet point + 1 vivid detail
OPINION (30 sec)   → Feelings + recommendation

Prep trick: Write 5 keywords only — not full sentences.

Part 3 — ARERC

Step What examiners want at Band 7
Answer Clear position (yes/no/depends)
Reason 1–2 reasons
Example Your country, people in general, news
Consequence Result, or balanced second view

Band 7 Part 3 rule: Say people, society, governments — not only I like / I don't like.

Part 1 — detailed tricks

Band 6.5 answer (too short)

Q: Do you use public transport a lot? A: Yes, sometimes I take the bus.

Band 7.0 answer (target)

A: Yes, I'd say I use it quite regularly, especially when I commute to work. The bus system in my city is fairly affordable, so it's a practical option when traffic is bad. That said, I still drive on weekends because it's more convenient for shopping.

→ Direct answer + frequency + reason + small contrast = Band 7 length and structure.

Wh-question patterns

Type Band 7 pattern
Do you…? Yes/no + how often + why
What kind…? Name type + describe + preference
When did you last…? Time + place + what happened + feeling
Why…? Main reason + for example + result
How often…? Frequency + routine + whether it will change

Useful Part 1 phrases (Band 6.5–7.0)

  • To be honest… / Honestly speaking…
  • I'd say… / I suppose…
  • It really depends, but generally…
  • The main reason is…
  • For instance… / For example…
  • On the other hand…
  • I used to…, but nowadays…

Part 1 connectors (push 6.5 → 7.0)

Link ideas with: because, so, however, although, which means, as a result

Example: I enjoy reading, which helps me relax, although I don't always have time.

Part 2 — detailed tricks

Band 6.5 problems

  • Stops at 60–90 seconds
  • Misses 1–2 bullet points
  • Lists facts with no feelings at the end

Band 7.0 fixes

  • Speak ~2 minutes
  • Touch every bullet on the cue card
  • End with opinion: I felt… / I would recommend… / Looking back…

Cue card types — what to say

Type Structure Band 7 must-have
Person Who → appearance → personality → story → influence One specific memory
Place Where → atmosphere → activities → sensory detail What you see/hear/smell
Thing What → how you got it → features → emotional value Why it matters to you
Past event When/where → sequence (first/then/finally) → lesson Past tense throughout
Activity How often → how you do it → benefits let off steam, future plan
Future trip Where/when → who with → plans → excitement I can hardly wait…

Keep talking (if you run out at 1:30)

  1. Compared to other places I've been…
  2. Most people I know would say…
  3. In the past it was different because…
  4. I'd definitely recommend it because…
  5. If I get the chance again, I will…

Part 2 timing

Time What to cover
0:00–0:15 Introduction
0:15–1:15 Bullets 1–3 with details
1:15–1:45 Bullet 4 + extra detail
1:45–2:00 Feelings + wrap-up

Part 3 — detailed tricks

Band 6.5 answer

Q: Should governments spend more on public transport? A: Yes, I think so. It's good for people. Traffic is bad in my city.

Band 7.0 answer

A: Yes, I believe governments should invest more in public transport, because it can significantly reduce traffic congestion and air pollution. For example, in my city, since the new metro line opened, thousands of commuters no longer need to drive, which has made rush hour slightly more bearable. As a result, I think both the environment and people's quality of life would improve if funding increased further.

Seven question types

Type Band 7 approach
Opinion Position → 2 reasons → example
Evaluation How important → justify → compare
Future Prediction → why → evidence/trend
Cause & effect Main reason + second reason
Hypothetical If X → then Y → example
Compare A vs B → which is better and why
Other people Many people… However…

Part 3 phrases

Buying time: That's an interesting question… / I haven't really thought about that, but…

Balanced (good for 7.0): It really depends… / On the one hand… on the other hand… / It's a double-edged sword.

Society level: In my country… / Many young people… / Governments should… / From a social perspective…

Band 6.5 → 7.0 checklist

Fluency - [ ] Part 1: no answer under 2 sentences - [ ] Part 2: speak 1:45–2:00 minimum - [ ] Part 3: at least 4 sentences per answer - [ ] Use fillers naturally: well, actually, you know

Vocabulary - [ ] 1–2 collocations per answer (traffic congestion, let off steam, worth every penny) - [ ] Paraphrase the question in your first line - [ ] See vocabulary tables

Grammar - [ ] One complex sentence per answer: relative clause or conditional - [ ] Self-correct once, then continue — don't apologise repeatedly

Pronunciation - [ ] Stress content words; don't speak in a flat monotone - [ ] Clear enough that examiner never asks you to repeat

Common mistakes at Band 6.5

Problem Fix
Part 1 too short Add because + example
Part 2 stops early Use "keep talking" list above
Part 3 only about "I" Add people in general, in my country
Memorised scripts Learn frameworks, adapt to question
Wrong tense in Part 2 Past story = past tense only
Forced idioms 1 per part maximum — natural only

Practice each topic as a connected set: Part 1 warms you up → Part 2 is the monologue → Part 3 extends the theme.

Topic 1: Transport

Simon key ideas: public transport reduces pollution; traffic congestion; cars more comfortable but cause congestion; investment in buses/trains eases traffic.

Part 1

Q1: Do people in your city use public transport a lot?

Band 6.5 Band 7.0
Yes, many people take the bus because driving is difficult. Yes, quite a lot of people rely on public transport, especially during rush hour when traffic congestion is terrible. In my area, the bus and metro are pretty affordable, so they're a practical choice for daily commuting. However, many families still prefer cars for convenience at weekends.

Q2: What would you like to change about public transport in your city?

Band 6.5 Band 7.0
I want more buses because they are often crowded. I'd like to see more frequent services, because at the moment buses are often overcrowded and sometimes unreliable. For instance, during peak hours people are packed like sardines, which is quite uncomfortable. If the government invested more, I think more people would leave their cars at home.

Q3: Do you think you will use public transport more in the future?

Band 6.5 Band 7.0
Yes, probably. It is cheaper. Yes, I think so, mainly because fuel prices are rising and parking is a hassle in the city centre. I'm actually planning to use the metro more when the new line opens next year. That said, I'll still drive when I travel with family.

Part 2 — Cue card

Describe a time when you were stuck in a traffic jam.

You should say: - When and where it happened - How long you were in the traffic jam - What you did while waiting - And explain how you felt about the experience

Band 7.0 outline (prep keywords):

When/where: last Tet holiday, highway to hometown
How long:   3 hours, 20km stretch
What did:   listened to podcast, chatted with family
Feeling:    frustrated but also bonding time

Band 7.0 sample (opening + middle):

I'd like to talk about a time when I was stuck in a terrible traffic jam during Tet holiday last year. It happened on the main highway leading to my hometown — pretty much everyone was travelling at the same time.

We were stuck for about three hours in a queue that barely moved. I was with my parents and younger sister, and to kill time we listened to music and chatted about family news. At first I felt really frustrated because I hate wasting time, but after a while I kind of accepted it — it's just part of the holiday experience in Vietnam.

Looking back, it wasn't enjoyable, but it made me realise how serious congestion is, and now I try to travel earlier or use the train when possible.

Part 3

Q: Which form of transport is more popular in your country?

Band 7.0: Motorbikes are still the most popular form of transport in Vietnam, especially in cities, because they're affordable and easy to park in narrow streets. However, cars are becoming more common among middle-class families, which actually makes congestion worse. Public transport like buses and metro is growing in big cities, but it hasn't replaced private vehicles yet.

Q: Do you think people need to change how they travel to protect the environment?

Band 7.0 (ARERC): Yes, I think people need to change their habits to some extent. The main reason is that private vehicles produce a large amount of carbon emissions, which contributes to air pollution. For example, in Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City, smog is often visible during rush hour. If more people switched to buses, metro, or even bicycles for short trips, the air quality would improve noticeably. Of course, governments also need to provide better infrastructure — people won't change if public transport is inconvenient.

Topic 2: Technology & Social Media

Simon key ideas: connects people globally; access to information; addiction risk; privacy concerns; face-to-face communication declining.

Part 1

Q1: Do you think you spend too much time on social media?

Band 6.5 Band 7.0
Sometimes yes. I use Facebook too much. Honestly, I think I do sometimes, especially in the evening when I'm tired and just scroll without thinking. I mainly use Facebook and Instagram to keep up with friends, which is useful, but it can easily become a waste of time. Recently I've been trying to limit it to about 30 minutes a day.

Q2: What do people often do on social media?

Band 6.5 Band 7.0
They chat and watch videos. Well, it varies, but most people use it to chat with friends, share photos, and watch short videos. Many also follow news and celebrities. In my country, social media has become a major source of information, although not everything online is reliable.

Q3: Do you prefer face-to-face or social media to communicate?

Band 6.5 Band 7.0
Face-to-face is better because it's more real. I prefer face-to-face communication for serious conversations, because you can read body language and emotions more clearly. However, social media comes in handy when friends live far away — for example, I video-call my cousins abroad every week. So I'd say both have their place.

Part 2 — Cue card

Describe a useful app on your phone/tablet/computer

You should say: - What it is - How often you use it - What you use it for - And explain why it is useful to you

Band 7.0 sample excerpt:

I'm going to describe an app I use every day called Grab, which is basically a ride-hailing and delivery platform.

I probably open it four or five times a week, mainly to book rides when the weather is bad or when I'm carrying heavy shopping. I also use it for food delivery when I'm too busy to cook.

It's incredibly useful because it saves me from the hassle of finding parking, and the prices are usually reasonable. Without it, my daily life in the city would be much less convenient — I honestly can't imagine going back to the old way.

Part 3

Q: How has social media changed the way people communicate?

Band 7.0: Social media has made communication much faster and more convenient — people can contact anyone instantly regardless of distance. However, it's also made interactions more superficial in some cases, because emoji and short messages replace deeper conversations. In my view, young people especially rely on online communication, which can reduce face-to-face social skills over time.

Q: Should children be allowed to use social media?

Band 7.0 (ARERC): It really depends on their age, but I wouldn't recommend unsupervised access for young children. The main concern is that they can be exposed to inappropriate content or cyberbullying. For example, many parents in my country now set time limits and use parental controls. If children use social media responsibly with guidance, it can help them learn digital skills — but unrestricted access is risky.

Topic 3: Education

Simon key ideas: education reduces poverty; technology in classrooms; studying abroad; class size; discipline; university vs vocational training.

Part 1

Q1: Do you have a favourite teacher?

Band 6.5 Band 7.0
Yes, my high school English teacher. She was kind. Yes, my high school English teacher left a strong impression on me. She was patient and always encouraged us to speak, even when we made mistakes. I think that's why I became more confident in English — she made the subject enjoyable rather than stressful.

Q2: What kinds of teachers do you like?

Band 6.5 Band 7.0
I like friendly teachers who explain clearly. I prefer teachers who are knowledgeable but also approachable — someone who can explain complex ideas in a simple way. Strict teachers don't bother me as long as they're fair. I also appreciate teachers who use real-life examples, because that helps me remember things better.

Part 2 — Cue card

Describe a lesson you remember well

You should say: - What the course was about - Where you had it - Why you remember it - And explain why it impressed you a lot

Band 7.0 sample excerpt:

I'd like to talk about a biology lesson in grade 10 that I still remember vividly. It was at my high school in Da Nang, in a small lab classroom.

We were learning about the human digestive system, and instead of just reading the textbook, our teacher brought in models and did a simple experiment. Everyone was engaged because we could actually see how things worked.

What impressed me most was her teaching style — she made a boring topic fascinating. That lesson changed how I viewed science, and it's probably one reason I became interested in research later on.

Part 3

Q: What rules should students follow at school?

Band 7.0: Students should follow basic rules like attending class on time, respecting teachers, and not disrupting others. I also think honesty in exams is essential. In my country, uniforms and phone restrictions are common, which some students dislike but I think help maintain discipline. Rules should be strict enough to create a good learning environment but not so rigid that they kill creativity.

Q: Do you think technology will replace teachers in the future?

Band 7.0 (ARERC): I don't think teachers will be fully replaced, although technology will definitely change their role. Online platforms can deliver content efficiently, but they can't provide emotional support or adapt to individual students the way a good teacher can. For instance, during COVID, many students struggled with motivation when learning alone at home. As a result, I see technology as a tool that supports teachers rather than something that eliminates them.

Topic 4: Environment

Simon key ideas: global warming; reduce waste; recycling; government responsibility; individual actions matter but are not enough alone.

Part 1

Q1: Do you like plants?

Band 6.5 Band 7.0
Yes, I like trees and flowers. They are beautiful. Yes, I do — I find them calming and they make any space feel fresher. We have a few pot plants at home, mainly succulents because they're low-maintenance. I think being around greenery helps me relax after a long day staring at a screen.

Q2: Do you like going to parks or public gardens?

Band 6.5 Band 7.0
Yes, I go sometimes to walk and relax. Yes, I enjoy visiting parks, especially early in the morning when it's quiet. In my city there are a few public gardens where families exercise and elderly people chat — the atmosphere is quite peaceful. I probably go once or twice a month, usually with friends or family.

Q3: Do you think governments should provide more public gardens?

Band 6.5 Band 7.0
Yes, because green space is good for health. Yes, I think they should, because urban areas are becoming increasingly crowded and people need places to exercise and unwind. Public gardens also improve air quality and give children a safe place to play. In my hometown, I'd say we don't have enough green space compared to the population.

Part 2 — Cue card

Describe an important plant in your country

You should say: - What the plant is - How you know it - Why it is important - And explain how you feel about it

Band 7.0 sample excerpt:

I'm going to describe rice, which is probably the most important plant in Vietnam.

Everyone here knows rice from childhood — it's in every meal and every market. I grew up seeing rice paddies whenever we visited the countryside.

It's important because it's our staple food and the backbone of agriculture — millions of farmers depend on it for their livelihood. Rice culture is deeply connected to Vietnamese identity and festivals.

Personally, I feel proud of it — when I travel abroad and miss home, a bowl of rice instantly reminds me of family dinners.

Part 3

Q: What can individuals do to protect the environment?

Band 7.0: Individuals can take several practical steps: reduce plastic use, recycle waste, save electricity, and use public transport when possible. For example, many young people in my city now bring reusable cups and bags. These actions seem small, but if millions of people do them, the collective impact is significant.

Q: Why is government involvement necessary in environmental protection?

Band 7.0 (ARERC): Government involvement is necessary because environmental problems are too large for individuals alone to solve. Industries that pollute on a massive scale need regulation and enforcement — a single person can't stop factory emissions. For instance, my government has introduced plastic bag charges and invested in solar energy projects. Without policy and funding at the national level, progress would be very slow.

Topic 5: Family

Simon key ideas: family size shrinking; working parents; divorce rates; caring for elderly; family vs career balance.

Part 1 (common questions — practise these)

Q1: Do you spend much time with your family?

Band 6.5 Band 7.0
Yes, I live with my parents and see them every day. Yes, quite a lot — I live with my parents and younger brother, so we have dinner together most evenings. Weekends are usually family time too, although work sometimes gets in the way. I think staying close to family is important in Vietnamese culture.

Q2: Who are you closest to in your family?

Band 6.5 Band 7.0
My mother because she understands me. I'm probably closest to my mother, because she's always been supportive and easy to talk to. We discuss everything from work stress to future plans. My father is caring too, but he's quieter — he shows love through actions rather than words.

Q3: Has your family changed much in recent years?

Band 6.5 Band 7.0
Not much. My brother went to university. Not dramatically, but my brother left for university two years ago, so the house feels quieter. My parents are also getting more comfortable with technology — my mum now uses Zalo video call daily. I'd say we're as close as before, just our routines have shifted.

Part 2 — Cue card

Describe a time when you felt proud of a family member

You should say: - When it happened - Who the person is - What the person did - And explain why you felt proud of him/her

Band 7.0 sample excerpt:

I'd like to talk about a moment two years ago when I felt extremely proud of my younger brother.

He was in grade 12 and preparing for the university entrance exam — one of the most stressful periods for Vietnamese students. For months he studied diligently without complaining, waking up at 5 a.m. every day.

When the results came out, he got into his dream university with a high score. I felt proud not just because of the result, but because I saw how much effort and discipline he showed. It motivated me to push myself harder in my own work too.

Part 3

Q: When would parents feel proud of their children?

Band 7.0: Parents typically feel proud when children achieve something through hard work — good exam results, graduating, getting a stable job, or even showing good character like honesty and kindness. In Vietnamese culture, academic success is especially valued, but I think parents are increasingly proud of independence and life skills too.

Q: Should parents reward children? Why and how?

Band 7.0 (ARERC): Yes, but in moderation. Rewards can motivate children and make them feel appreciated when they achieve something. For example, a small gift or a family meal out after a good report card is reasonable. However, if parents reward children too often for basic behaviour, they may become dependent on external motivation. I think praise and quality time are often more meaningful than material rewards.

Q: Is it good to reward children too often?

Band 7.0: No, I don't think so. If children are rewarded for everything, they may not develop intrinsic motivation — they only act when there's a prize. It can also create unrealistic expectations. A balanced approach works better: recognise real achievements, but also teach children that responsibility is normal, not something extra to be paid for.
